History of Coffee

The legend

Legend has it that Kaldi, a goat-herder from Ethiopia in the 9th century went out to search for some of his missing goats, only to find them energetically dancing and eating the berries of a small tree which he had never seen before. Wondering what could be the cause of these effects on the goats he decided to try the berries himself, feeling a rush of vitality come to him a little while after eating the berries. After this beautiful experience, he became determined to show this new great discovery to the world.


And so, Kaldi brought the berries to a nearby monastery, but the monks disapproved of this rare fruit fearing that its effects could be related to demons or evil spirits. The monks then proceeded to throw the berries into the fire, from which an exquisite smell poured out that caused nearby people to come and see what was that fragrance.


Thinking that such a great aroma could not be related to any evil entity, the roasted beans were removed from the fire by the monks then ground and put into water, making it the first cup of coffee in the history of the world.


This legend may sound like a fairy tale and possibly not the real deal, but it surely is an interesting approach to this beverage’s origins and there should be some truth behind it, such as the discovery of the energizing effect of coffee by someone mistakenly eating those berries.

Varieties

There are two main varieties of coffee beans: Arabica and Robusta. The primary distinctions lie in the altitude at which they thrive and their immunity to pests, particularly in terms of caffeine content. Arabica, being more sensitive and less resistant, typically flourishes at higher altitudes, starting from approximately 1200 meters above sea level.

These are just a few of most popular specialty coffee varieties:

Typica

It is one of the two parent varieties of coffee cultivars, selectively bred for over 500 years. Typica offers a sweet, mild, and approachable flavor. Notes of chocolate and nut take center stage, complemented by hints of spice. Typica's acidity is mild and balanced, conjuring dried fruit and dark stone fruit like black cherry and plum.

Bourbon

Named after an island off the coast of Madagascar, is culturally and genetically important. A parent of numerous coffee varieties, Bourbon delivers brown sugar-like sweetness, fruit notes of cherry and peach, and a round body.

Caturra

A genetic mutation of Bourbon, first identified in a village called Caturra in Brazil, is cultivated for its smaller plant size and higher production. Similar to Bourbon but somewhat brighter in taste.

Gesha

This Ethiopian variety, selected from the Gori Gesha forest, is highly valued for rarity, production costs, and flavor. Light, bright, and floral, with tea-like characteristics not usually found in coffee.

SL28

It is among the most well-known African varieties, first identified in Kenya, is known for grapefruit-like brightness, savory flavor similar to sundried tomato, and a syrupy body.

Coffee Brewing Methods

Espresso

It is a concentrated coffee brewing method where pressurized hot water is forced through finely-ground coffee. It results in a strong, rich, and flavorful shot of coffee, commonly used as a base for various coffee beverages.

French Press (Press Pot or Plunger Pot)

In this brewing, coarse coffee grounds are steeped in hot water, and a plunger with a mesh filter is used to separate the grounds after brewing. This method produces a full-bodied and robust cup of coffee.

Aeropress

This method involves brewing coffee by immersing grounds in water and then using air pressure to push the brewed coffee through a filter. It's known for its versatility and the ability to produce a clean cup of coffee.

Pour-Over

This brewing involves pouring hot water over coffee grounds in a slow, controlled manner. This method allows for precise control over brewing variables and is appreciated for highlighting the nuanced flavors of the coffee.

Cold Brew

It is made by steeping coarse coffee grounds in cold water for an extended period, often 12-24 hours. The result is a smooth, less acidic, and highly caffeinated coffee concentrate that is typically diluted before consumption.

Turkish Coffee

In this brewing method, finely ground coffee, water, and sugar (optional) are combined and brewed in a special pot called a cezve. The coffee is heated without boiling and served unfiltered in small cups, grounds and all.

Siphon

This brewing method uses a vacuum process to brew coffee. Water is heated in the lower chamber, and as it rises, it mixes with coffee grounds. After brewing, a vacuum effect draws the brewed coffee back down, leaving the grounds behind.
